Introduction#

Most things change, and we are already quite familiar with discussing how quickly they do so in our day-to-day lives. The formal study of time-dependent systems is called “mathematical dynamics”. In this book we will look at how we can describe rates of change using equations that involve the derivative. We will also look at some techniques for analysing and interpreting rate of change equations.

Describing change#

To describe change we use the language of calculus because it allows us to write down mathematical models that govern the behaviour of systems. By investigating these models, we often can gain insights about the systems they represent. It all starts with the first derivative, which defines the rate of change.

For a given quantity \(x\),

  • if \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t>0\) then \(x\) is increasing

  • if \(\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t<0\) then \(x\) is decreasing

In many scenarios the rate of change may depend on time \(t\) or on the value of \(x\) itself.

Exercise 1

Water flows out of a bucket at a constant rate of 0.1L/sec. Write down an equation for the amount of water \(V\) left in the bucket at time \(t\). Is it realistic?

Solution

Let \(V(t)\) be the amount of water (in litres) left in the bucket at time \(t\) (in seconds). According to the given information,

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{\mathrm{d}t}=-0.1 \quad \Rightarrow V(t)=V_0 - 0.1t \end{equation*}\]

It works until the bucket is empty!

However, the rate of outflow may depend on water level in the bucket (e.g. due to pressure)

Exercise 2

Newton’s second law says that Force=mass \(\times\) acceleration.

Can you write this down as a problem involving velocity \(v\)?

Solution

We use the fact that acceleration is the rate of change of velocity to write result in mathematical form as:

\[\begin{equation*} F=m\frac{\mathrm{d}v}{\mathrm{d}t} \end{equation*}\]

We may also write the equation in terms of the displacement, using the fact that the velocity is the rate of change of displacement:

\[\begin{equation*} F= m \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}\left(\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}\right)=m\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 x}{\mathrm{d}t^2} \end{equation*}\]

Dotty notation

The derivatives that we will be looking at represent changes with respect to time. It is convential to use “dotty” notation to represent time derivatives in the following manner:

\[\begin{equation*} \dot{x}=\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}, \qquad \ddot{x}=\frac{\mathrm{d}^2x}{\mathrm{d}t^2} \end{equation*}\]
