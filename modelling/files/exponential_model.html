1. Exponential model#

The exponential model is used in situations where we expect the relative growth rate to remain constant.

1.1. Discrete model#

Before we write down the governing differential equation for exponential growth, let us first consider the case of discrete compound interest. We may describe this model using a difference equation involving discrete steps \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta t\).

Exercise 1.1

A loan company charges interest of \(r\%\) per month. Show that the loan amount is goverened by a difference equation of the following form:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{r}{100}. \end{equation*}\]

Also write down the general solution given an initial investment of \(x_0\)

Solution

In each time step \(\Delta t=1\)

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right)x-x}{1}=\frac{r}{100}x. \end{equation*}\]

The equation may be rearranged to give the claimed result. The general solution is

\[\begin{equation*} x=x_0\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right)^t, \qquad t=0,1,2,\dots \end{equation*}\]

Exercise 1.2

Given a model of discrete compound growth of a quantity \(x\) with growth factor \(r\) and initial value \(x_0\), find the time to reach a given value.

Solution

\[\begin{align*} \frac{x}{x_0}=(1+r)^t \quad &\implies \quad \ln(x/x_0)=t\ln(1+r)\\ & \implies t=\frac{\ln(x/x_0)}{\ln(1+r)} \end{align*}\]

1.2. Continuous model#

If interest is compounded continuously then it is convenient to write

(1.1)#\[\begin{equation} x=x_0 e^{\lambda t}. \end{equation}\]

Differentiating then gives the model for exponential growth:

(1.2)#\[\frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=\lambda\]

According to the model, the relative growth rate is constant. In the case where \(x\) represents a population we can interpret this as the “per-capita” growth rate.

Doubling and half-life

We could also express the equation in relation to the doubling time \(\tau\). For \(x\) to be doubled, \(x/x_0=2\) which gives

(1.3)#\[\begin{equation} e^{\lambda\tau}=2 \quad \implies \quad x=x_0 2^{t/\tau} \end{equation}\]

A similar result can be derived for the half-life.

Exercise 1.3

Cocaine has a half-life of around 0.7-1.5 hour in human blood. Determine what percentage will remain after six hours.

Solution

Let \(\tau\) be the half-life. Then,

\[\begin{equation*} x=x_0\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{t/\tau} \end{equation*}\]

When \(t=6\)hrs,

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{x_0}= \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{6/\tau} = [0.26 - 6.25]\%. \end{equation*}\]

You may wish to compare this to the results for caffeine and alcohol, which each have a mean half-life of about 5 hours.

Discrete vs continuous

the continuous model is related to the discrete model by taking

(1.4)#\[\lambda =\ln\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right)\]

Exercise 1.4

Derive the result given in (1.4) by equating the discrete and continuous models.

Solution

Equating the two models gives

\[\begin{equation*} x_0\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right)^t=x_0 e^{\lambda t} \end{equation*}\]

Cancelling a factor of \(x_0\) on both sides and noting that \(e^{\lambda t}=(e^{\lambda})^t\) we find that

\[\begin{equation*} e^{\lambda}=\left(1+\frac{r}{100}\right) \end{equation*}\]

Taking the natural logarithm gives the stated result.

1.3. Offset model#

In an offset exponential model, the rate of change of a quantity \(Q\) is proportional to the difference from some equilibrium value \(Q_e\) in the manner

(1.5)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}Q}{\mathrm{d}t}=r(Q_e-Q) \end{equation}\]

The equilibrium value is the point where \(\dot{Q}=0\). If the system reaches its equilibrium then it will remain there unless some external influence acts on the system to move it away from its equilibrium.

Notice that if \(r>0\) then

  • when \(Q\) exceeds the equilibrium value the growth rate will be negative

  • When \(Q\) is less than the equilibrium value the growth rate will be positive

Therefore this system “wants” to go to the equilibrium value.

Exercise 1.5

By defining \(D=(Q_e-Q)\), write down an equation for \(\dot{D}\) and solve it.

Solution

Let \(D=(Q_e-Q)\), then

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{\mathrm{d}D}{\mathrm{d}t}=-rD \quad \implies \quad D=D_0 e^{-r t} \end{equation*}\]

i.e

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{Q-Q_e}{Q_0-Q_e}=e^{-rt} \end{equation*}\]

Exercise 1.6

Apply the offset equilibrium model to find the time taken for a drink to cool from 80C to 63C given an ambient (room) temperature of 20C, given that \(r=0.11\)/min.

Solution

\[\begin{equation*} \ln\left(\frac{Q-Q_e}{Q_0-Q_e}\right)=-rt \quad \implies \quad t=\frac{-1}{0.11}\ln\left(\frac{63-20}{80-20}\right)=\frac{-1}{0.11}\ln(43/60) \end{equation*}\]

The drink will take 3 mins and 2 sec to reach the desired temperature.

Note that according to this model, the drink will never actually reach room temperature, though it will approach it in the limit.

1.4. Varying rate model#

We can extend the exponential model to account for the case where the relative growth rate is not constant, but can be described by a known function \(f(t)\) :

(1.6)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} = f(t), \qquad x_0=x(t_0) \end{equation}\]

The solution to this equation can be shown to be given by:

(1.7)#\[\begin{equation} x= x_0 e^{\int_{t_0}^t{f(t)\mathrm{d}t}} \end{equation}\]

Exercise 1.7

Find the solution to the following problem, which describes the Gompertz survival function:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=-\beta e^{\alpha t}, \qquad x(0)=x_0 \end{equation*}\]

Solution

In this case, \(f(t)=-\beta e^{\alpha t}\). Integrating with respect to \(t\) gives:

\[\begin{equation*} \int_0^t f(t)\mathrm{d}t = \biggr[-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}e^{\alpha t}\biggr]_0^t=\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\biggr(1-e^{\alpha t}\biggr) \end{equation*}\]

Therefore the problem solution is given by:

\[\begin{equation*} x=x_0e^{\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\biggr(1-e^{\alpha t}\biggr)} \end{equation*}\]
