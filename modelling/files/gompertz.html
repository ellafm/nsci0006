3. Gompertz model#

The Gompertz model is another S-shaped function. It is derived from different assumptions to the logistic function, and has somewhat different properties. Other S-shaped functions include the Weibull and Fréchet functions.

3.1. The model#

The model is defined by an exponentially decaying growth rate:

(3.1)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=\beta e^{-\alpha t} \end{equation}\]

This equation can be satisfied by taking:

(3.2)#\[\begin{equation} x=e^{-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}e^{-\alpha t}} \end{equation}\]

3.2. Human mortality#

The Gompertz model has been applied to model the survival rates in human populations as a function of age, by assuming that the number of survivors decreases at mortality rate \(\mu\), which increases exponentially:

(3.3)#\[\frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=-\beta e^{\alpha t} \qquad \implies \qquad x=x_0e^{\frac{\beta}{\alpha}(1-e^{\alpha t})}\]

Parameters \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) can be esimated from data:

  • \(\beta\) is the baseline mortality, which can be estimated from the mortality rates at a very young age.

  • \(\alpha\) is the (exponential) rate of increase of mortality with age

An example of how the Gompertz survival function looks is shown below. In the plot we have taken \(x_0=100\) to illustrate the projected outcomes for an initial population of 100 individuals.

The modal age of death occurs when \(D=\mathrm{d}x/\mathrm{d}t\) is maximised. It is the point where the survival curve has a point of inflection. We can find the age when this occurs.

According to the model (3.3),

(3.4)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 x}{\mathrm{d}t^2}=-\beta x e^{\alpha t}\left[\alpha-\beta e^{\alpha t}\right] \end{equation}\]

Therefore we find that the modal age of death occurs when

(3.5)#\[\begin{equation} \alpha - \beta e^{\alpha t}=0 \quad \implies \quad \tau=\frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\left(\frac{\alpha}{\beta}\right) \end{equation}\]

Using the parameters given in in the above figure and taking account of they 50 year offset, we obtain the following results for Japanese men and women:

  • \(\frac{1}{0.113}\ln(0.113/0.001)+50 =91.84\) (women)

  • \(\frac{1}{0.098}\ln(0.098/0.003)+50 =85.57\) (men)

We can also express the model (3.3) in terms of the modal age, resulting in a form which may be easier to interpret:

(3.6)#\[\begin{equation} x=x_0 e^{-\alpha (t-\tau)} \end{equation}\]

3.3. Classical form#

By taking \(m=x_0 e^{\frac{\beta}{\alpha}}\), we may rewrite the solution to (3.3) as follows:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{m} = e^{-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}e^{\alpha t}} \qquad \implies \qquad \ln\biggr(\frac{x}{m}\biggr)=-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}e^{\alpha t} \end{equation*}\]

Therefore the problem (3.3) may be rewritten in the following implicit form, which is common in the literature:

\[\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=\alpha \ln\biggr(\frac{x}{m}\biggr) \end{equation*}\]
