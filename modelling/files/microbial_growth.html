4. Microbial growth#

In this chapter we look at another S-shaped function, which is used to model the growth rate of micro-organisms on a substrate.

4.1. Background#

The growth rate of a microorganism on a substrate demonstrates an S-shaped curve consisting of a “lag” phase (flat) followed by exponential growth, followed by a “stationary” (flat) phase.

To estimate the relative growth rate during the exponential phase we can fit a straight line to the logged data, as illustrated in the plot below. However, in this chapter we will outline a model that can be used to fit the entire growth curve.

4.2. Cell yield#

Taking \(x\) and \(S\) to be the cell mass and substrate concentration at time \(t\), we define the cell yield as the rate of cell mass production per unit of substrate consumed:

(4.1)#\[Y= = \frac{\dot{x}}{-\dot{S}}=-\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}S}\]

It is a constant, which depends on the substrate structure and the micro-organism. You may verify by differentiation that the equation is satisfied by either of the following relationships, illustrating that substrate concentration decreases linearly with cell mass:

(4.2)#\[S=S_0-\frac{1}{Y}(x-x_0), \qquad S=\frac{C}{Y}\left(1-\frac{x}{C}\right)\]

where

  • \(S=S_0\), \(x=x_0\) are the initial substrate concentration and cell mass

  • \(S=0\), \(x=C\) are the substrate concentration and cell mass at carrying capacity

Solution

For example, for the first result confirm that \((S_0,X_0)\) satisfies the equation. Differentiation with respect to \(t\), treating \(Y\) as a constant yields:

\[\begin{equation*} \dot{S} = -\frac{1}{Y}\dot{x} \quad \implies Y=\frac{\dot{x}}{-\dot{S}} \end{equation*}\]

4.3. Monod’s model#

The Monod equation is an empirical relationship that can be used to model microbial growth mathematically by taking into account the effect of substrate concentration \(S\). According to the model,

(4.3)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{1}{x}\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=\frac{\mu_{max}S}{K_s+S} \end{equation}\]

where \(\mu_{max}\) and \(K_s\) are constants.

Note

In this model, the relative growth rate is usually called the “specific growth rate”. The constant \(\mu_{max}\) is the maximum specific growth rate and \(K_s\) is called the “half-saturation constant”. When \(S=K_s\) the specific growth rate is at half its maximum value.

4.4. Chemostat model#

A chemostat is a bioreactor in which the cell substrate is continuously diluted so that the specific growth rate of the microorganism can be controlled. This can be modelled by using an adjusted form of Monod’s model, featuring the dilution rate \(D\):

(4.4)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t}=(\mu-D)x, \qquad \mu=\frac{\mu_{max}S}{K_s+S} \end{equation}\]

Note that at steady state (equilibrium) \(\mu=D\). This gives

(4.5)#\[D_{crit}=\frac{\mu_{max}S}{K_s+S}\]

If \(\mu>D\) the utilisation of the substrate will exceed supply, causing the growth rate to slow until it is equal to the dilution rate.

If \(\mu<D\) the amount of substrate will exceed the amount utilised, so the growth rate will increase until it is equal to the dilution rate.

Explanation

Since \(\dot{S}=-\dot{x}/Y\) it follows that if \(\dot{x}>0\) then \(\dot{S}<0\). This makes sense since bacterial growth implies substrate utilisation. It can also be shown that if \(\dot{S}<0\) then \(\dot{\mu}<0\) and so the growth rate will slow.

A similar (reverse) argument applies to the case where \(\dot{x}>0\).

Efficiency and washout

A bioreactor is most efficient when the growth rate \(\mu\) is maximised. We can see from (4.5) that in equilibrium the bioreactor will be most efficient at higher dilution rates.

However, if the dilution rate is increased too far then the system will not be able to reach equilibrium without the growth rate exceeding \(\mu_{max}\). The microbes can no longer reproduce fast enough to maintain a population, and so they wash out of the tank and the reaction ceases.
